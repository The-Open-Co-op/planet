# PLANET Messaging Framework
## v0.1 — March 2026

---

## 1. The Core Challenge

Every early adopter in this space has a graveyard of projects they were excited about. Diaspora, Solid, Mastodon, Ello, Scuttlebutt, Urbit, Holochain. Each one said "we're different." Most died or stayed tiny. The people we need to reach are immune to manifestos. They've been burned by vision without execution.

**No words will convince them. Only evidence will.**

The landing page is not a sales pitch. It's a presentation of evidence, followed by an invitation: "Given all this, do you want in?"

---

## 2. What PLANET Actually Is

PLANET is three things:

- **An app** — messaging, photo vault, verified public pages, trusted introductions
- **A network** — invite-only, everyone is real, trust builds through normal social behaviour
- **A co-op** — owned by members, funded by members, directed by members

No incumbent offers all three. Facebook is an app and a network but is owned by shareholders and monetises your attention. Signal is an app with good privacy but has no network effects beyond messaging and no member governance. Open Collective is transparent funding but not a consumer product. PLANET combines all three.

### The One-Liner

> **"PLANET is a private digital home that you actually own."**

When someone asks "what do you mean, own?" — that opens the conversation: "It's a cooperative. Members fund what gets built, vote on decisions, and see where every penny goes. No shareholders, no ads, no one mining your data."

### The Elevator Pitch (30 seconds)

"PLANET is an app, a network, and a co-op. It's where your photos, messages, and communities live — private, encrypted, and owned by the people who use it. Everyone on PLANET was invited by someone real. There are no bots, no ads, no algorithms. Members fund what gets built and vote on every decision. We launch in September with 80,000 organisations already mapped in the regenerative economy. This isn't another startup — it's a collectively owned digital ecosystem."

## 3. Audience Segments & Messaging

### Audience A: The Invited (your mum)

**Where they come from:** A personal invite link from someone they trust.

**What they need:** Reassurance, not persuasion. They've already been invited — they just need to know it's safe.

**Touchpoint:** NOT the main landing page. They arrive via the invite acceptance flow (separate link/page).

**Key messages:**
- "[Name] invited you to PLANET"
- "It's a private space for your family and communities"
- "Your photos and messages belong to you, not Big Tech"
- "Download here. [Name] will walk you through it."

**Tone:** Warm, simple, zero jargon. Think: a letter from a friend, not a product page.

**This audience does NOT see the main landing page unless they choose to learn more.** The invite flow handles them entirely.

---

### Audience B: The Early Adopter

**Who they are:** Technically aware, values-driven, already using Signal/Mastodon/Proton. Probably working on their own project. Have seen hundreds of "we'll fix the internet" pitches. Deeply sceptical but actively searching for something that actually works.

**What they need:** Evidence this is real, not vapourware. Proof of traction. A clear reason why THIS project will succeed where others failed.

**Key messages:**
- "You've seen a hundred projects that promised to fix the internet. Here's why this one's different."
- "80,000 organisations already mapped. A funded technology stack in active development. Twenty years of cooperative infrastructure."
- "This isn't a startup looking for an exit. It's a cooperative — you own it, you direct it, you can see where every penny goes."
- "Members fund the features they want. See what's being built right now."

**Evidence points that matter to them:**
- CoBot's 80,000 nodes (real, searchable, verifiable)
- Murmurations' 30,000 discoverable organisations
- First Person Collective stack in active development by a funded team
- Open Collective transparency (live financial data)
- Real community organisers already committed (names and communities)
- Feature bounties with real money pledged

**What they're looking for that we must NOT do:**
- Don't claim to be "the one" that unites everything
- Don't lead with ideology over substance
- Don't hide behind jargon (trust graph, VRCs, DIDs)
- Don't show mockups without explaining what's real today vs. what's planned

**Tone:** Honest, direct, slightly understated. Confident but not arrogant. "We've been doing this work for twenty years. Here's what we've built. Here's what's coming. Judge for yourself."

---

### Audience C: The Community Organiser

**Who they are:** Running a Transition Town, food co-op, mutual aid network, community energy project, or similar. Currently dependent on Facebook Groups, WhatsApp, or email lists. Has been burned by platform changes. Cares about member ownership and governance.

**What they need:** A practical alternative to Facebook Groups that their members will actually use, with real governance and data sovereignty.

**Key messages:**
- "Run your community on infrastructure your members own"
- "No Facebook, no WhatsApp, no platform risk"
- "Your member directory, your communications, your governance — all in one place"
- "Your community is already on our map — claim it and bring it to life"

**The CoBot angle:** If their organisation is in CoBot, they can search for it, see it on the map, and "claim" it as a founding community on PLANET. This turns a passive directory listing into an active onboarding hook.

**Tone:** Practical, empathetic, peer-to-peer. "We know what it's like to run a community on borrowed infrastructure. Here's something better."

---

### Audience D: The Builder / Developer

**Who they are:** Developer, designer, or technical community leader. Looking for interesting infrastructure to build on. Motivated by open protocols, cooperative economics, or just interesting technical challenges.

**What they need:** Technical credibility, open protocols, and a viable ecosystem (i.e., actual users to build for).

**Key messages:**
- "Build apps on a trust graph with real, verified humans"
- "Open protocols, open source, no platform lock-in"
- "Members fund development through feature bounties — build what people actually want and get paid"
- "VTA plugin architecture — snap your app into every community on the network"

**Tone:** Technical but not dry. Show the architecture. Link to repos. Make it clear this is real code, not a whitepaper.

---

### Audience E: The Movement Person

**Who they are:** Feels something is deeply wrong with the current system. May be involved in climate justice, cooperative economics, degrowth, commons, mutual aid. Wants to be part of building an alternative, not just critiquing the existing one.

**What they need:** A vision they can believe in AND evidence it's achievable. They've been disappointed before.

**Key messages:**
- "The extractive internet isn't inevitable. We're building the alternative."
- "This isn't just an app — it's the beginning of a member-owned digital economy."
- "80,000 organisations. Thousands of communities. One cooperative infrastructure."
- "Join the founding generation."

**Tone:** Fire tempered by substance. Visionary but grounded. "We're not dreaming about this — we're building it. Here's the evidence."

## 4. Leveraging CoBot

### Current State
- 80,000 nodes (organisations in the regenerative economy)
- Searchable, categorised, with location data
- No direct contact details for most organisations
- Not currently a PLANET feature, but a strategic asset

### How to Use It

**On the landing page: The search hook.**

Embed a CoBot search on the PLANET landing page: "Find your organisation." When someone from a Transition Town or community energy project searches and finds their org already mapped, it creates a powerful moment of recognition. CTA after finding it: "Be the first to bring [org name] to PLANET" or "Claim your community's place."

This serves two purposes: it proves the ecosystem is real (80,000 organisations, searchable), and it creates a lead generation mechanism that doesn't require email lists. The community organiser self-identifies by searching for their org.

**As evidence on the page.**

A live counter: "80,247 organisations mapped in the regenerative economy." Possibly a subtle animated map showing density. This isn't claiming they're "on PLANET" — it's showing that PLANET is being built for a real, mapped, existing ecosystem.

**Via network admins (the distribution channel).**

You know community organisers and network administrators. Each one connects to dozens or hundreds of the 80,000. The approach:
1. Identify 15-20 network admins you have direct relationships with
2. Show them the CoBot data for their network: "Here are the 400 organisations in your network that we've mapped"
3. Ask: "Which 5-10 of these would be most interested in owning their own community infrastructure?"
4. Those admins become your recruitment channel

**As the onboarding bridge.**

When PLANET launches, community onboarding starts with "Find your organisation on CoBot" → "Claim it" → "Set up your community on PLANET." For Murmurations orgs, profile data pre-populates. CoBot becomes the bridge between the mapped ecosystem and the living network.

### Honest Framing

CoBot is a directory, not a community. "80,000 organisations mapped" is truthful. "80,000 organisations on PLANET" is not. Early adopters will see through inflation instantly, and credibility lost is almost impossible to rebuild.

## 5. Open Collective Bounty System

### Why Open Collective

Building a custom funding platform would take months and nobody would trust it initially. Open Collective provides:
- Radical financial transparency (every transaction visible to anyone)
- Goals with progress tracking (balance-based or yearly budget)
- Tiers for different contribution levels
- Established trust and reputation in the open source community
- GraphQL API for pulling live data into the PLANET landing page
- Handles payments, tax receipts, fiscal hosting

### Setup

**Collective:** Use existing Open Co-op collective or create PLANET-specific one.

**Goals (Feature Bounties):**

Each goal represents a fundable feature. Examples for launch:

| Goal | Target | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| WhatsApp Chat Import | £5,000 | Bring your family chats to PLANET |
| Sovereign AI Assistant | £15,000 | AI on your vault data, no surveillance |
| Community Governance Tools | £8,000 | Proposals, voting, decisions |
| Offers & Wants | £6,000 | Trust-verified local exchange |
| LinkedIn Contact Import | £3,000 | Bring your professional network |
| Family Shared Calendar | £3,000 | Shared calendar in the family vault |

**Tiers (Membership Levels):**

| Tier | Price | Benefits |
|------|-------|----------|
| Waitlist | Free | Priority invite, founding member status |
| Supporter | £30/year | 50GB vault, co-op voting rights, name in charter |
| Pioneer | £90/year | 1TB vault, early API access, builder community |
| Community | £150/year | Community space, founding community status |

### Integration with Landing Page

Query Open Collective GraphQL API to display live on the PLANET site:

```graphql
query {
  account(slug: "planet") {
    name
    stats { totalNetAmountReceived { value currency } }
    members(role: BACKER) { totalCount }
    tiers {
      nodes {
        name
        goal { amount { value } }
        stats { totalAmountReceived { value } contributorsCount }
      }
    }
  }
}
```

Display as:
- Live progress bars for each feature bounty
- Total members counter
- Total raised counter
- Recent contributions feed (social proof)
- "Every penny transparent" link to Open Collective page

### Why This Works

An early adopter lands on the page. They see "WhatsApp Import — 73% funded — £3,650 of £5,000 — funded by 84 members." That's not a promise. It's a verifiable fact. They can click through to Open Collective and see every transaction. This is proof that:
1. Real people are paying real money
2. The project is transparent about finances
3. Members genuinely direct development
4. This isn't vaporware — it's happening

No other "fix the internet" project can show this on their landing page because none of them operate this way.

## 6. Landing Page Architecture

### Primary Audience: Early Adopters
### Secondary: Community Organisers, Builders, Movement People
### NOT on this page: The Invited (handled by invite flow)

### Structure

**Nav:** PLANET logo | Why | What | Fund | Build | Join

**Hero**

The honest opening. Not a claim — a challenge.

> Headline area (exact copy TBD, but in this territory):
> "You've seen a hundred projects that promised to fix the internet."
> "We didn't start with a whitepaper. We started with 80,000 organisations, a funded tech stack, and twenty years of building cooperative infrastructure."
> "Now we're opening the door."

Two CTAs:
- [Join the Waitlist] — primary
- [I've Been Invited] — routes to invite acceptance flow

Live counter: "X members joined | £X raised | 80,247 organisations mapped"

**Section 1: The Problem (brief)**

Keep the four pain points from the existing page — they work. But compress. No more than a screen height. The early adopter already knows the problem; they need to see you know it too, then move on quickly to the solution.

**Section 2: What You Get**

Not a feature list — a set of outcomes. Each one is a card:
- "Your photos, backed up and private" — Personal vault with photo backup
- "Messages with real people only" — E2EE messaging, invite-only network
- "A verified presence that's actually yours" — First Person Pages, DID-signed
- "Introduce people you trust" — The introducer, PLANET's signature mechanic
- "Communities you own and govern" — Real cooperative community spaces
- "You decide what gets built next" — Feature bounties, transparent budget

**Section 3: The Evidence**

This is the section that makes PLANET different from every other pitch. Three columns:

**The Ecosystem is Real**
- CoBot search embed: "Find your organisation"
- "80,000+ organisations mapped in the regenerative economy"
- "30,000 discoverable via Murmurations"
- Logos/names of committed founding communities

**The Technology is Real**
- "Built on the First Person Collective stack"
- "In active development by a funded team"
- "Open source. Open protocols. No lock-in."
- Links to repos (when public)

**The Money is Real**
- Live Open Collective data: total raised, total members
- Feature bounty progress bars (pulled from OC API)
- "Every penny transparent" — link to full OC page
- Recent contributions feed

**Section 4: The Co-op**

Brief but important. "PLANET is a cooperative, not a startup."
- One member, one vote
- Members fund features directly
- Budget fully transparent
- No venture capital, no shareholders, no exit strategy
- "You're not signing up for a service. You're joining a movement."

**Section 5: For Builders**

Shorter technical section:
- Open protocols, VTA plugin architecture
- Feature bounty marketplace (build what members fund)
- SDKs: Rust, Dart, TypeScript
- "The first ecosystem where users pay developers directly"
- Link to technical docs

**Section 6: Join**

Tier cards (from Open Collective):
- Waitlist (Free) — priority invite, founding status
- Supporter (£30/year) — vault storage, voting rights
- Pioneer (£90/year) — everything + builder access
- Community (£150/year) — community space

Email capture for free tier. Payment via Open Collective for paid tiers.

Social proof: member counter, recent joins, testimonials from founding community organisers (when available).

**Footer**

"PLANET is operated by The Open Co-op. Every penny transparent. Every decision democratic."
Links: Open Collective | CoBot | Murmurations | First Person Collective | GitHub

## 7. What's NOT on the Landing Page

- Technical jargon (DIDs, VRCs, VTA, trust graphs) — save for builder docs
- Detailed feature specifications — the page shows outcomes, not architecture
- AI promises — add when it's genuinely good, not before
- Comparisons to specific competitors — let the proposition speak for itself
- The word "decentralised" — early adopters are tired of it; show don't tell
- Exaggerated claims about the 80,000 orgs — they're mapped, not members

## 10. Tone & Voice Guidelines

**PLANET speaks like:** A knowledgeable friend who's been doing this work for years. Not a startup founder. Not a preacher. Not a salesperson.

**Always:**
- Honest about what exists today vs. what's planned
- Specific rather than abstract (numbers, names, dates)
- Confident but not grandiose
- Respectful of the audience's intelligence and scepticism

**Never:**
- "Revolutionary" / "disruptive" / "paradigm shift"
- "Decentralised" as a selling point (show it, don't say it)
- Promises without evidence
- Jargon without explanation
- Attacking specific companies by name (let the contrast speak)

**The voice test:** Would you say this sentence to someone at a conference who just asked "what are you working on?" If it sounds like a pitch deck, rewrite it. If it sounds like an honest conversation, keep it.
